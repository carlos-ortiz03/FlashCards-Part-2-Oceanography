Expedition 349: The Objectives

Posted on January 16, 2014 by geologelizabeth

IODP Expedition 349 is designed to address questions about seafloor spreading and paleo-environment in the South China Sea. From a geophysical viewpoint, the South China Sea is fascinating, because it’s undergone almost a full Wilson Cycle.

The Wilson Cycle is an umbrella term describing the process of an ocean basin opening and closing. This process controls the development of the continents. It “begins” – if one can fix a point where a circle begins – with continental rifting: two tectonic plates pull apart. New magma rises from below the thinning crust to replace it. The magma cools, and forms new rock. As the continents continue to pull apart over millions of years, the growing basin fills with water and becomes an ocean. At the other side of the plate, oceanic crust collides with land. Because oceanic crust is dense with iron and magnesium, while the continent crust contains more silica, the ocean crust subducts (squashes and sinks) below the continent. Progressive sinking of the ocean crust below the continent ultimately closes the basin, and, throughout the millennia, the cycle continues. Hence, the Earth never grows or shrinks; it simply reconstructs & recycles itself.

Illustration of the Wilson Cycle, from Press et al., Understanding Earth, 4th ed. 2003

The South China Sea has the added complexity of being the junction between not just 2 tectonic plates, but 3: the Pacific plate, the Eurasian Plate, and the Indo-Australian plate. Our chief scientists, Dr. Chun-Feng Li and Dr. Jian Lin, are interested in figuring out when the South China basin began to open.

To determine the age of the South China Sea basin, Expedition 349 is cruising to three locations to collect basement rock from the sea floor. We’re also going to collect all the sediment layered up on top of it. Knowing the age of the overlying sediment will bracket the age of the rock beneath it.

That’s why I’m along. I’m a paleontologist. My job is to look at the fossils in the sediment core as they’re brought up on deck, and – hopefully in agreement with my fellow paleontologists as well – give our colleagues an on-the-spot idea of where we are in time. And my forensic dating tools are a microscopic marine plankton called foraminifera. Stand by to hear more about forams in my next post!
